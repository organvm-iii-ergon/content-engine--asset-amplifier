# Two-Corpus Formation And Closure Governance Report

## Input Boundary

This report interprets two prompt groups as a combined lifecycle:

1. The earlier brainstorm/project-formation corpus, which asks for naming,
   purpose, academic rigor, computability, algorithms, architecture,
   experiments, market translation, and next artifacts.
2. The later closeout/governance corpus, which asks whether the session is safe
   to close, whether work was saved and issued, whether the IRF and interested
   indices were updated, whether `N/A` indicates a vacuum, whether additive
   history was preserved, whether local and remote are `1:1`, and whether any
   loss has been recovered.

The report is theoretical and interpretive. It does not perform a live closeout
audit.

## Executive Finding

The two corpora are separate macro entities inside one repeatable lifecycle.
The first is a formation sequence: it turns an idea into a rigorous, testable,
buildable, and market-translatable system. The second is a closure-governance
sequence: it proves the work survived, propagated, and can be safely left.

The missing bridge between them is a traceability spine: a durable ledger that
connects each produced artifact to its location, purpose, obligation, registry
surface, remote persistence, open questions, and closure evidence.

## Macro Sequence

1. **Meta-intake**
   Preserve raw language, classify prompt fragments, separate provenance from
   authority, and decide whether the bundle contains one sequence or multiple
   macro entities.

2. **Formation**
   Name the system, define purpose, formalize the core object, ground it in
   sources, define models and algorithms, establish proof or experiment paths,
   architect execution, choose operating depth, and translate for markets.

3. **Traceability spine**
   Record what was produced, where it lives, why it exists, what obligation it
   satisfies, what remote or registry surface knows about it, and what remains
   unresolved.

4. **Closure governance**
   Audit what was done, detect missing state, verify additive history, confirm
   local-remote parity, propagate to applicable external indices, recover any
   loss, and issue a safe-to-close verdict.

## Formation Micro-Sequence

1. **Name / compression**
   Create a memorable symbolic handle for the transformation.

2. **Purpose**
   Define audience, problem, transformation, boundary, and success condition.

3. **Core object**
   Turn the central concept into a computable object with fields, invariants,
   constraints, and update rules.

4. **Source grounding**
   Build the canonical source ladder, academic pillars, methodological
   standards, and constraint domains.

5. **Formal model**
   Define variables, state transitions, attribution, scoring, and system
   ontology.

6. **Algorithms**
   Specify extraction, scoring, ranking, attribution, generation, and failure
   handling.

7. **Proof / rigor**
   Map claims to invariants, estimators, experiments, benchmarks, or bounded
   failure analysis.

8. **Experiments**
   Define hypotheses, controls, treatments, metrics, windows, and acceptance
   criteria.

9. **Architecture**
   Translate the proven or bounded mechanism into repo structure, services,
   schemas, workflows, environment variables, and deployment surfaces.

10. **Operating depth**
    Choose lean MVP, operator-grade, dissertation-grade, or production-grade
    depth.

11. **Market translation**
    Produce pitch, one-pager, technical spec, landing page, or other
    audience-specific expression.

12. **Next artifact**
    Select the next artifact by dependency order rather than excitement.

## Closure-Governance Micro-Sequence

1. **Whole-session overview**
   State all that was, all that is, and all that still needs to be.

2. **Adversarial audit stance**
   Act as the hall-monitor looking for broken rules, missing propagation, and
   false closure.

3. **Registry obligation**
   Check completed and discovered work against the relevant registry surfaces,
   including universal work registries, GitHub issues, scorecards, logs,
   concordances, seeds, and architecture docs where applicable.

4. **Vacuum detection**
   Treat `N/A`, no data, missing seed, missing link, or unknown state as a
   possible obligation to research, plan, or log.

5. **Additive-history check**
   Confirm that work was appended, versioned, or archived correctly and that
   prior state was not improperly overwritten.

6. **Persistence parity**
   Verify local and remote survival. The doctrine is `local:remote = 1:1`: if
   the physical manifestation dies, the remote record should preserve the work.

7. **Loss response**
   If anything was lost, recovery comes before ordinary progress.

8. **Final propagation**
   Commit relevant artifacts, push to the correct remote branch, and update all
   applicable interested indices.

9. **Closure verdict**
   End with a direct safe-to-close, unsafe-to-close, or conditionally-safe
   judgment backed by evidence.

## What Should Repeat

- Use the meta-intake sequence whenever a messy prompt bundle is pasted.
- Use the formation sequence whenever a new project, product, theory, or system
  is being born.
- Use the traceability spine whenever an artifact is created or changed.
- Use the closure-governance sequence at every session boundary.
- Use the adversarial audit stance whenever the question is whether work
  survived, propagated, or can be trusted.

## What Stays Where

- Formation prompts stay in the project-formation method.
- Closeout prompts stay in the closure-governance method.
- Traceability belongs between them, not at the end only.
- The generic pasteboard meta-process should classify both entities before
  sequencing their internal parts.
- The closeout method should wrap the formation method; it should not be
  reduced to a final optional documentation step.

## Missing Pieces

- **Artifact ledger**: every produced artifact with path, purpose, status,
  owner, and downstream obligation.
- **Interested-index matrix**: which registries, issues, logs, scorecards,
  seeds, architecture docs, or concordances apply by work type.
- **Vacuum ledger**: every `N/A`, no-data field, missing link, unresolved
  claim, or absent seed requiring follow-up.
- **Additive-history rule**: when to append, version, archive, or replace.
- **Closure verdict format**: safe, unsafe, or conditionally safe, with cited
  evidence and remaining risks.

## Repeatable Prompt Templates

### Meta-Intake Prompt

```text
Treat this information bundle as a meta-assignment. Preserve raw language,
separate provenance from authority, classify each item by function, expected
output, and value, identify separate macro entities, sequence the micro-items
inside each entity, extract reusable processes, and stage the critical argument
for what interpretation should win.
```

### Formation Prompt

```text
Turn this project idea into its strongest formation sequence: name it, define
purpose and audience, formalize the core object, ground it in sources, define
models and algorithms, map claims to proof or experiments, choose architecture
only after the mechanism is coherent, translate it for markets, and select the
next artifact by dependency order.
```

### Traceability Prompt

```text
For every artifact or decision produced in this session, create the traceability
spine: path, purpose, originating ask, satisfied obligation, downstream
dependency, registry or issue link, remote persistence state, open vacuums, and
closure evidence required before the session can be called safe.
```

### Closure-Governance Prompt

```text
Review the whole session as an adversarial closeout auditor. State all that was,
all that is, and all that still needs to be. Verify saved artifacts, additive
history, local:remote parity, GitHub/issues/registry/index propagation, missing
N/A vacuums, and recovery needs. Treat absence as a possible obligation, not as
a neutral blank. End with a direct safe-to-close, unsafe-to-close, or
conditionally-safe verdict supported by evidence.
```

## Critical Take

The formation corpus creates knowledge and possible systems. The closure corpus
prevents that knowledge from evaporating into a session transcript. The first
without the second overproduces artifacts without survival guarantees. The
second without the first becomes procedural policing without generative content.

The strongest combined method is therefore cyclical: form the system, attach
traceability as it forms, then close adversarially. Closure is not bureaucracy
after creation; it is how creation becomes durable.

## Conversation Arena

**Defense:** The two-corpus lifecycle is strong because it separates creative
formation from survival proof while keeping them connected through traceability.

**Objection:** The lifecycle risks becoming too heavy, especially for early
brainstorms where a full registry and closeout audit may slow exploration.

**Outside view:** The right weight depends on artifact consequence. A disposable
scratch note needs light traceability. A repo, registry, client-facing artifact,
or cross-session process needs full closure governance.

**Judging criterion:** The winning interpretation is the one that preserves raw
voice, produces useful generalized prompts, keeps formation and closure distinct,
and makes loss or false closure harder.

5. How should future prompt bundles signal whether they are asking for live
